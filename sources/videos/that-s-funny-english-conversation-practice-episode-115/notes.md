# That's Funny! | English Conversation Practice | Episode 115

## Summary

This EnglishPod episode introduces vocabulary for jokes, comedy performance, and simple English wordplay. The dialogue is set at an open mic night, where a comic performs short jokes for an audience that does not respond warmly. The core vocabulary includes "open mic night", "comic", "stand-up comedy", "routine", "zinger", and "heckler". These words let learners describe a live comedy setting, the performer, the prepared set of jokes, and audience behavior.

The lesson also explains why several short jokes work. Some are visual jokes, such as a sheep with no legs looking like a cloud. Others rely on English sound patterns, especially "movies" sounding like "moo-vies" in the cow joke and "dam" sounding like "damn" in the fish joke. This makes the episode useful for learners who understand dictionary meanings but miss humor because of pronunciation, homophones, or cultural expectations. The closing note that shared jokes should be "clean" adds register awareness: jokes may be funny in one setting but inappropriate in another.

## Knowledge Map

- Comedy venue vocabulary: open mic night, comic, stand-up comedy.
- Performance vocabulary: routine, zinger, short jokes.
- Audience reaction vocabulary: tough crowd, hecklers.
- Wordplay mechanics: animal sounds, homophones, and double meanings.
- Register control: clean jokes versus swearing or inappropriate humor.

## Key Takeaways

- "Open mic night" is an event where amateurs can perform for an audience.
- A "comic" performs stand-up comedy, often using a prepared routine.
- A "heckler" interrupts or criticizes from the audience.
- Many English jokes depend on sound, not just literal meaning.
- "Clean jokes" are safer in public, classroom, or workplace settings.
