# Mechanic | English Conversation Practice | Episode 117

## Summary

This EnglishPod episode teaches car-repair and mechanic vocabulary through a breakdown scenario. A driver brings a car to a mechanic after it starts spewing white smoke and dies on the road. The mechanic asks the driver to pop the hood, then explains that the white smoke is steam from the radiator and that the overheated engine has damaged the pistons and transmission. The repair will require spare parts, work on the electrical system, fuel pump, and engine, and at least two weeks in the shop.

The lesson combines technical car words with highly practical everyday phrases. Learners get informal language such as "busted" for broken and "die on me" for something stopping while in use. They also learn mechanic-shop interaction patterns: "What seems to be the problem?", "How long is this going to take?", "How much does it cost?", and "Can you check my car?" The hosts' discussion adds real-life breakdown stories involving a dead transmission, a flat tire, and asking whether someone needs a hand. The extra practice section reinforces beginner mechanic vocabulary such as engine, tire, oil change, repair shop, flat tire, strange sound, and price questions.

## Knowledge Map

- Car-problem reporting: spew white smoke, die on me, does not start, strange sound.
- Repair-shop interaction: what seems to be the problem, how long will it take, how much does it cost.
- Car parts: radiator, pistons, transmission, fuel pump, electrical system.
- Informal broken-language: busted, dead, shot.
- Repair process: tow, order spare parts, take apart, put back together.

## Key Takeaways

- "Busted" is common informal English for broken.
- "Die on me" means something stopped working while I was using it.
- "Pop the hood" means open the front engine cover of a car.
- "Spare parts" are replacement parts used for repairs.
- "Take apart" means remove the pieces; "put back together" means reassemble them.
