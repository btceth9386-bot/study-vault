# Expire as an Everyday Validity Verb

- **One-sentence definition**: To **expire** means to stop being valid, usable, safe, or accepted after a certain date or time.
- **Why it exists / what problem it solves**: English uses "expire" for many everyday things: coupons, credit cards, driver's licenses, passports, memberships, contracts, and food. If you know this verb, you can understand warnings and explain why something can no longer be used.
- **Keywords**: expire, expired, expires, valid, invalid, date, renewal

## Summary

"Expire" is the word English uses when time runs out on something. A coupon is useful before its expiration date, but after that date the cashier will not accept it. A driver's license can be legal today and expired next month. Food can also expire, meaning it may no longer be safe or fresh.

The grammar is simple: "This coupon expired yesterday," "My license expires next month," and "The milk has expired." The idea is always the same: there was a time limit, and after that limit the thing loses its normal use.

## Example

At a supermarket:

> Customer: "I also have a couple of coupons."
> Cashier: "Sir, these coupons expired yesterday."

Other everyday examples:

> "My driver's license expires in a month, so I need to renew it."
> "Don't use that card. It expired five years ago."
> "Don't drink that milk. It has expired."
