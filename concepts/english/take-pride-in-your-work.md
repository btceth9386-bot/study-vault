# Idiom: Take Pride in Your Work

- **One-sentence definition**: To work carefully and thoroughly so you feel genuinely satisfied with the result — also used as polite criticism when someone is being careless.
- **Why it exists / what problem it solves**: This phrase has two faces: (1) a positive value ("I take pride in my work" = I care about quality), and (2) a gentle rebuke ("You've got to take pride in your work" = you're being sloppy, do better). Missing this dual meaning can cause you to misread workplace conversations.

## Summary

"Take pride in your work" means doing your job well enough that you can look at the result and feel good about it. But context changes everything:

- **Self-description**: "I take pride in my work" → positive, means "I care about quality"
- **Said to someone else**: "You've got to take pride in your work" → gentle criticism, means "you're being careless, step it up"

In the lesson, a worker left splinters on the wood (didn't sand it properly). Another worker says "You got to take pride in your work" — meaning "this isn't good enough, do it right."

This also connects to a broader English pattern: **"take + noun"** for actions:
- Take pride (feel proud)
- Take care (be careful)
- Take responsibility (own the outcome)
- Take charge (lead)

## Example

From the lesson:
> "I think you should sand the edges. Look at all these splinters. Someone could get hurt. Jeez, you got to take pride in your work."

The speaker isn't complimenting — they're saying "this is sloppy, someone will get hurt, do better."

Contrast with self-use:
> "I always take pride in my work — I never ship code without testing it."

## Relationship to other concepts

- [[rule-of-thumb-idiom]]: Both are workplace wisdom phrases. "Rule of thumb" gives the guideline; "take pride" gives the motivation to follow it.
- [[construction-tools-vocabulary]]: The idiom appears in the construction context — not sanding wood properly triggers the criticism.
- [[give-110-percent-idiom]]: Both describe work attitude; "give 110 percent" stresses effort, while "take pride" stresses quality.
- [[up-to-code-phrase]]: Pride is a personal quality standard, while code is an external safety or legal standard.
